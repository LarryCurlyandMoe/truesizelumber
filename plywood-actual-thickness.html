Sheet Goods, Same Trick

"3/4" plywood" isn't 3/4" thick either.

Plywood and OSB are sold by nominal thickness, same as dimensional lumber — but the gap between nominal and actual is usually smaller, and it varies more by manufacturer than dimensional lumber does. A "3/4 inch" sheet is commonly closer to 23/32", though this isn't as tightly standardized as the 2x4-style lumber chart.

Most "¾ inch" plywood actually measures around 23/32" (≈0.70") — roughly 1/32" thinner than nominal. The exact figure varies by manufacturer, panel grade, and species.

Why these numbers are "typical" rather than exact: unlike dimensional lumber (2x4, 2x6, etc.), which follows one consistent national standard, plywood actual thickness varies more by manufacturer, panel grade, and whether it's sanded or unsanded. Some modern panels are now manufactured to metric equivalents (matching mm sizes used internationally) rather than the traditional fractional-inch targets shown above. Always measure your specific sheet with calipers before cutting anything that needs a precise fit — a stair stringer notch, a flush-mount panel, or a groove that needs to match a specific board thickness.

Why plywood shrinks less than dimensional lumber

Plywood is made from thin wood veneers glued together in layers (called plies), rather than milled from a single solid piece of wood. It still goes through sanding, which removes a small amount of material from nominal thickness — but it doesn't go through the same drying-shrinkage process as solid dimensional lumber, since the veneers are already dried before they're glued up. That's the main reason the nominal-to-actual gap is smaller for sheet goods than it is for something like a 2x4.

Where this actually matters on the job

The thickness difference is small, but it adds up in a few common situations:

  • Underlayment and flush transitions — stacking nominal vs actual thicknesses across a doorway transition can leave you slightly off from a perfectly flush finish.
  • Dado and groove joints — if you cut a groove sized for "3/4 inch" plywood using a 3/4" router bit, the panel will be loose in the slot, since the panel itself runs thinner than the bit.
  • Shelving and cabinet specs — manufacturer cut lists and cabinet hardware are often designed around actual sheet thickness, not the nominal label.

For anything where a tight, precise fit matters, the safest move is the same advice as with dimensional lumber: measure the actual sheet in front of you rather than trusting the label printed on it.
